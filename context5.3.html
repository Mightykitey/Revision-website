5.3 - Technical Change and Management

5.3.1 - Understand the factors that trigger change in organisations:

Changes within organisations can occur for a variety of reasons. These reasons are typically classified into planned and unforeseen factors:

  • Planned for factors: These are changes that the organisation expects and plans for in advance. They can include new features or functionalities being added to products or services, diversification into new markets, rebranding efforts, the adoption of new technologies, or reacting to competition in the market.
  • Unforeseen factors: These changes are unexpected and can arise from external or internal shocks, such as a crisis (e.g., financial meltdown), cyber attacks, system failures, or other unanticipated events that require immediate response and adaptation from the organisation.

5.3.2 - Understand the technical change management process:

The technical change management process helps organisations systematically implement changes to their systems, processes, or infrastructure:

  • Identifying the changes to be made: The first step is recognizing what changes need to be implemented, whether it’s upgrading a system, implementing a new technology, or altering existing processes.
  • Communicating risks and impacts to stakeholders: It is essential to communicate the potential risks and impacts of the change to all relevant stakeholders. This includes assessing and addressing how the change will affect operations, employees, customers, and partners.
  • Configuration and implementation of the system/process: After identifying the changes and risks, the next step is configuring the system or process for the change and implementing it. This may involve technical adjustments, setting up new tools or software, and ensuring proper integration.
  • Importance of rollback planning and reproducibility: It’s crucial to have a plan in place to revert the system to its previous state if the change leads to unforeseen issues. Rollback planning ensures that the change process can be reversed if necessary. Reproducibility ensures that the change can be repeated or replicated if needed in other areas of the organisation.

5.3.3 - Understand how organisations respond to, prepare for, and manage change:

Organisations must be able to respond quickly and effectively to various types of changes. These can include:

  • Economic changes: Shifts in the economy, such as inflation or recession, can impact business strategies, product pricing, and cost management.
  • Banking and financial changes: Changes in financial regulations, interest rates, or market trends can affect organisational operations, especially in financial services.
  • Environmental changes: Environmental factors, such as climate change or sustainability concerns, may require changes in operations or product offerings.
  • Legal and political changes: Changes in laws or regulations, such as new privacy laws or political shifts, may require organisations to adapt quickly to stay compliant.
  • Social changes: Shifts in social norms, demographics, or customer preferences may require organisations to adjust their strategies to remain relevant.
  • Technological changes: The rapid pace of technological advancements may force organisations to update their IT infrastructure or adopt new technologies to stay competitive.

5.3.4 - Understand the benefits and drawbacks of technical change:

While technical changes bring numerous benefits, they also come with potential drawbacks. Understanding both is essential for effective change management:

  • Productivity: New technologies or systems can improve productivity by automating tasks, enhancing collaboration, and streamlining workflows. However, if not implemented correctly, they can disrupt daily operations.
  • Communication: Technical changes, such as adopting new communication tools, can improve interaction and collaboration within teams and with customers. However, the learning curve or compatibility issues might hinder initial communication.
  • Security: Improved security measures can be a significant benefit of technical change, protecting the organisation from cyber threats. However, vulnerabilities during the implementation phase can expose the organisation to security risks.
  • Costs: Implementing technical changes can incur costs, including software, training, and integration. While these costs can lead to long-term savings, they may present short-term financial challenges.
  • Stakeholder experience: Technical changes can improve the experience for customers and employees by providing better tools, services, or products. However, poorly managed changes can cause frustration, confusion, or resistance from stakeholders.
